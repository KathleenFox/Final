The Ideal Death

Music boxes are cursed portals into a forgotten land.

The demonic clanking melody that strums the air

when the deviant box is opened is the only remains

of sound after the land’s daffodil destruction. 

I clutched this clattering casket in my callous hands,

ripping the lid opened and close as if to simultaneously 

disrupt and continue the trembling tune.

Dogwoods tipped their hats to us in respect

as the wind mourned our procession with petals.

I was riding in the passenger seat of my aunt’s hearse,

not because there was no room in the posh limo, 

but because I had always wanted to ride in the corpse mobile

while alive. “Could you stop with the box?” Rick,

the death cabby, said to me frigidly. His eyes collapsed off

the road and the hearse crashed into a lime minivan

like a fruity blast of cadavers on a desolate hospital table.

The music box cracked.

Shards broke off from the mirror in the music box

that had more dust than glass. They pierced 

my eye and I saw this supposed lost world.

Rustic robotic trees stood stark against the gold wind,

and a boy, who I named Derek, had clocks for corneas.

He watched my spine spin under a hypothermic breeze

and smiled as the blood dripped from my eye.

I felt numb, like my body was shrouded in an iceberg.

My pulse was a shattered porcelain doll; flatline.

The box was truly a portal to another world, 

but I began to wonder if it was a forgotten one.

I see a hand on my shoulder, decorated

with cracked nails and rings as black

as a spider’s heart. I turn to see my aunt,

cloth in tattered tree silk, crying.

They say Hell is cold.
