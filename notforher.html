Not For Her

An exit sign illuminates the white wallpapered walls,

as the subway system shakes the building like a maraca.

Aunt Margie’s skin chokes on the heavy makeup

that was meant to liven her face, but seems to eat away at her flesh.

Michael and I kneel to pray.

I gawk at the body. Michael avidly avoids eye contact.

We rise from the pew left in front of the cupcake corpse

as the coffin stares us down when we offer our condolences.

Boys give handshakes.

Girls offer a kiss on the cheek.

Idly chatting in front of the idle corpse

while the coffin screams for the weeping water it deserves.

Unfamiliar relatives collect their prayer cards and wonder who’s next?

We talk of dresses, and vacations, forgetting all about the wilted heart.

Laughter echoes. No one cries.

Have we forgot how to mourn the woman

whose memory tore us away like tattered taffeta ball gowns?

Church bells give an angelic ode to the procession,

turning their chatter to silence, and their heads to the graven floor.

The coffin guides us through the hallowed aisles, practically grinning.

At last some attention! Prayers and passages exchange looks of order

and all is fine, until the eulogy tears down the white wallpapered wall,

and reminds us that Aunt Margie was a person

who loved so many, so dearly.

The tears escaped from the eyes and stained my face with a lash.

She is happy to be free of this demented disease, like a child watching

a bright kite flicker in the autumn breeze for the first time.

But she left so many behind to bask like lightning bugs without light.

The tears are not for her, but for those

who will no longer have lips smack their cheeks on both sides,

or remember her dazzling dresses that sparkled like her smile.

The tears were for her sisters, hiding their water droplets in laughter and

the cousin’s memory that lost the last lingering thought of who she was.

I wiped them away before they reached the finish line, but I

walked out of the church like a wave riding on a fleeting sunset.
