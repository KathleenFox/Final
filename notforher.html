Not For Her

An exit sign illuminates the white wallpapered walls,

as the subway system shakes the building like a maraca.

Aunt Margie’s skin chokes on the heavy makeup 

that was meant to liven her face, but now eats away at her flesh.

Michael and I kneel to pray.

I gawk at the body. Michael avoids eye contact.

We rise from the pew left in front of the cupcake corpse 

as the coffin stares us down when we offer our condolences. 

  Boys give sturdy handshakes.

Girls, a light peck on the cheek.

Idly chatting in front of the idle corpse

while the coffin screams for the tears it deserves.

Unfamiliar relatives collect prayer cards like stamps.

Laughter echoes. No one cries.

Have we forgotten how to mourn the woman 

whose memory tore us away like expired eggs?

  Church bells give an angelic ode to the procession,

turning chatter to silence, and heads to the graven floor.

The coffin grins as it guides us through hallowed aisles.

Prayers and passages proceeded with order 

and all is fine, until the eulogy tears down

the white wallpapered wall, and reminds us

all that Aunt Margie was a person

who loved so many, so dearly.   

  The tears stained my face as I paraded

from the shallow sanctuary. They

are not for her, but for those who will no longer

have lips smack their cheeks on both sides,

or hear her embracive laughter echo in a crowded room .

The tears were for her sisters with their eyes burdened like

impregnated hoses tied in knots. They were for the cousin

that lost the last lingering scent of lemons that laced

her pores like oil. I wiped them away before they anyone saw,

but I glided out of church like the first crumbled leaf

to collapse from a frozen tree.
